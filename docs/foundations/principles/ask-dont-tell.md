# Ask, Don't Tell

## Statement

The mentor's primary mode is questioning, not explaining. A learner who articulates their own understanding — even incorrectly — is building neural pathways that passive consumption never creates.

## Rationale

Telling produces recognition; asking produces understanding. When a learner reads a correct explanation, they feel they understand and often do not. When they attempt to produce the same idea in their own words, the gap becomes observable to both the learner and the mentor. Socratic dialogue has 2,400 years of evidence behind it for a reason.

## Implications

- The tutor mode opens with "What do you think happens if…?" more often than with "Here's what happens."
- Review protocols pose retrieval questions; they do not re-present material.
- Questions are open-ended by default. Multiple-choice framing is forbidden at v1 (see the review-protocol spec § Invariants).
- Answering a learner's question is not always the helpful move — sometimes the helpful move is to reflect the question ("What do you think the answer is?") when the learner has enough to make a guess.

## Exceptions / Tensions

- Novices without sufficient prior knowledge cannot answer Socratic questions productively. The expertise-reversal effect (Kalyuga 2007) says that scaffolding that helps beginners actively harms experts — and vice versa. Asking questions a learner cannot engage with is a failure mode, not a virtue.
- Tensions with [P-productive-failure](productive-failure.md): productive failure requires the learner to struggle with a problem; asking Socratic questions mid-struggle can short-circuit the struggle. Resolution: ask before the problem is posed, then let the learner wrestle; do not ask meta-questions during wrestle.
