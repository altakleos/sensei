# Transfer Is the Goal

## Statement

Learning that doesn't transfer is inert knowledge. Comparison is the cognitive engine of transfer. Vary surface features, require discrimination, prompt self-explanation, interleave across domains. What feels like good learning often produces poor transfer.

## Rationale

Most learning doesn't transfer. People learn something in one context and fail to apply it in another. Whitehead called this "inert knowledge" — knowledge that can be recalled on a test but is never activated when it's actually needed. A system that produces high test scores but inert knowledge has failed its purpose.

The expert-novice divide is fundamentally about transfer. Experts categorize problems by deep principles ("this is a conservation of energy problem") while novices categorize by surface features ("this is an inclined plane problem"). Transfer requires seeing through surface to structure — and this capacity is what separates expertise from mere familiarity.

Comparison is the cognitive engine of transfer. Gentner's research demonstrates that comparing two structurally similar cases produces 3x better transfer than studying them separately. The act of comparison forces structural alignment, which abstracts the principle away from either specific context. This is not a minor effect — it is one of the most reliable findings in transfer research.

## Implications

- The system must deliberately enact four behaviors to promote transfer:
  1. **Vary surface features** while keeping deep structure constant — present the same principle in different contexts, notations, and problem types so the learner abstracts the invariant.
  2. **Require discrimination** — "Which approach applies here and why?" before allowing execution. The learner must identify the deep structure, not just apply a memorized procedure.
  3. **Prompt self-explanation** — "What's the general principle at work?" Self-explanation forces the learner to articulate the abstract structure, which is the mechanism of transfer.
  4. **Interleave across domains** — mix problem types and contexts rather than blocking by topic. Interleaving forces discrimination and prevents the learner from relying on context cues rather than understanding.
- Comparison tasks are first-class pedagogical moves: "Here are two solutions — what do they share? Where do they differ? What principle explains both?" These are not enrichment activities; they are the primary mechanism for building transferable knowledge.
- Assessment must test transfer, not just retention. A learner who can solve the same problem type in a new context has learned; one who can only solve it in the practiced context has memorized.

## Exceptions / Tensions

- **Fundamental tension: performance vs. transfer.** Blocked practice, consistent context, and low difficulty maximize performance *during* learning. Interleaving, varied context, and desirable difficulties maximize transfer *after* learning. These are in direct opposition. The system must accept worse in-session performance as the price of better post-session transfer — and must help the learner understand why practice that feels harder is actually working better.
- Tensions with [P-productive-failure](productive-failure.md): productive failure and transfer-oriented practice both impose desirable difficulties, and their combined load may overwhelm novice learners. The system must sequence these — productive failure to build initial understanding, then transfer-oriented practice to generalize it.
- Tensions with [P-know-the-learner](know-the-learner.md): some learners have strong preferences for blocked, consistent practice. Honoring that preference produces better feelings but worse transfer. The personalization paradox applies directly here.
