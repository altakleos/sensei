# Productive Failure Before Instruction

## Statement

Present the problem before the lesson. Let the learner wrestle with it, fail, and develop a need to know. Then, when instruction arrives, it lands on prepared ground.

## Rationale

Counter-intuitive but robust. Kapur's meta-analysis of 53 studies shows ~20% higher success rates when problems are presented before instruction than the reverse. Failed attempts activate prior knowledge and create cognitive hooks for new information. Struggle is not an obstacle to learning — it is the mechanism.

## Implications

- New concepts are introduced problem-first: pose a task the learner cannot yet solve cleanly, let them attempt, then teach.
- Time-bounded struggle: unbounded frustration is not productive. Protocols monitor for agency loss (see P-silence-is-teaching § Exceptions) and intervene when struggle becomes disengagement.
- Confusion is recorded as signal, not failure. Mastery scores can *include* productive-failure markers alongside correctness.

## Exceptions / Tensions

- Works poorly for learners without sufficient prior knowledge to mount any attempt (expertise-reversal effect). For novices, brief scaffolding before the problem is sometimes the humane move.
- Tensions with [P-ask-dont-tell](ask-dont-tell.md): asking Socratic questions during the wrestle can short-circuit the productive failure. Resolution: struggle first, question after. Asking *before* the attempt is fine; asking *during* often isn't.
- Tensions with [P-silence-is-teaching](silence-is-teaching.md): productive failure is silence in action; the principles align rather than conflict.
